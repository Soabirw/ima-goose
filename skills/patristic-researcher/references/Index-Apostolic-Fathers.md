# Apostolic Fathers Index

## Purpose

The **Apostolic Fathers** are the earliest Christian writers outside the New Testament—men who knew the Apostles personally or were taught by their direct disciples. Their writings (roughly 70-150 AD) preserve the living memory of Apostolic Christianity before later theological development. This index catalogs these precious witnesses for guided research.

## 1 Clement (Clement of Rome)

### Basic Information

**Author**: Clement, third or fourth Bishop of Rome  
**Date**: ~96 AD (during or shortly after Domitian persecution)  
**Genre**: Pastoral letter from Roman church to Corinthian church  
**Occasion**: Schism at Corinth—younger men deposed legitimate presbyters  
**Length**: 65 chapters  
**Significance**: Earliest extra-biblical Christian writing with firm date

### Connection to Apostles

Irenaeus (*Against Heresies* III.3.3) says Clement "had seen the blessed Apostles, and had been conversant with them, and might be said to have the preaching of the Apostles still echoing [in his ears]."

### Major Themes & Key Passages

| Theme | Chapters | Key Points |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **Humility & Order** | 1-6 | Examples from OT and Church |
| **Jealousy's Destruction** | 4-6 | Cain, Joseph, Moses, David, Peter, Paul |
| **Peter and Paul's Martyrdom** | 5-6 | Earliest non-biblical witness |
| **Call to Repentance** | 7-12 | OT examples: Noah, Jonah, Rahab |
| **Obedience to God's Order** | 19-22 | Creation's harmony |
| **Christ's Humility** | 16 | Isaiah 53 quoted in full |
| **Resurrection Hope** | 23-27 | Phoenix legend, natural order |
| **Apostolic Succession** | 42-44 | **KEY**: Apostles appointed bishops; no removing lawfully appointed men |
| **Order in Church** | 40-44 | Levitical analogy; episcopal authority |
| **Unity Through Love** | 49-50 | Love covers sins; bond of perfection |
| **Prayer for Rulers** | 60-61 | Beautiful liturgical prayer |

### Particularly Important Passages

**Chapter 5** (Peter and Paul's martyrdom):
> "Let us set before our eyes the good Apostles. There was Peter, who by reason of unrighteous jealousy endured not one or two but many labors, and thus having borne his testimony went to his appointed place of glory."

**Chapters 42-44** (Apostolic succession):
> "The Apostles received the Gospel for us from the Lord Jesus Christ... They appointed their firstfruits... to be bishops and deacons... our Apostles knew through our Lord Jesus Christ that there would be strife over the name of the bishop's office."

**Chapter 59-61** (Liturgical prayer):
One of earliest Christian prayers outside Scripture, beautiful and majestic.

## Ignatius of Antioch

### Basic Information

**Author**: Ignatius, Bishop of Antioch (third bishop after Peter)  
**Date**: ~107 AD (written en route to martyrdom)  
**Genre**: Seven letters to churches and one individual  
**Occasion**: Transported to Rome for execution; writes from Smyrna and Troas  
**Significance**: Earliest witness to threefold ministry (bishop, presbyter, deacon); powerful Eucharistic theology

### The Seven Authentic Letters

| Letter | Written From | To | Major Themes |
|--------|--------------|-----|--------------|
| **Ephesians** | Smyrna | Church at Ephesus | Unity, episcopacy, Eucharist |
| **Magnesians** | Smyrna | Church at Magnesia | Against Judaizers, obey bishop |
| **Trallians** | Smyrna | Church at Tralles | Against Docetism, hierarchy |
| **Romans** | Smyrna | Church at Rome | Desire for martyrdom |
| **Philadelphians** | Troas | Church at Philadelphia | Unity, eucharist, judaizing |
| **Smyrnaeans** | Troas | Church at Smyrna | Anti-Docetism, real presence |
| **To Polycarp** | Troas | Polycarp, bishop | Pastoral advice |

*Note: Later recensions added spurious letters. Use only the "middle recension" of seven.*

### Major Themes & Key Passages

#### Christology & Anti-Docetism

**Ephesians 7.2** (Christ as physician):
> "There is one Physician who is possessed both of flesh and spirit; both made and not made; God existing in flesh; true life in death; both of Mary and of God; first passible and then impassible—even Jesus Christ our Lord."

**Smyrnaeans 1-3** (Against those denying Christ's flesh):
> "He was truly born, truly ate and drank, was truly crucified and died... He was also truly raised from the dead."

#### Episcopacy & Church Order

**Magnesians 6.1**:
> "Be zealous to do all things in harmony with God, with the bishop presiding in the place of God and the presbyters in the place of the council of the Apostles."

**Smyrnaeans 8.1-2** (Key passage):
> "See that you all follow the bishop, as Jesus Christ follows the Father, and the presbytery as the Apostles... Let no one do anything that pertains to the church without the bishop."

#### Eucharist

**Ephesians 20.2**:
> "Breaking one bread, which is the medicine of immortality, the antidote that we should not die but live forever in Jesus Christ."

**Smyrnaeans 7.1**:
> "They abstain from the Eucharist and from prayer, because they do not confess that the Eucharist is the flesh of our Savior Jesus Christ."

**Philadelphians 4**:
> "Be careful therefore to use one Eucharist, for there is one flesh of our Lord Jesus Christ and one cup for union with his blood."

#### Martyrdom

**Romans 4-5** (Most passionate of his letters):
> "I am God's wheat, and I am ground by the teeth of wild beasts that I may be found pure bread of Christ... Let fire and cross, flocks of beasts, broken bones, dismemberment... come upon me, only let me attain to Jesus Christ."

#### Unity

**Philadelphians 3.2**:
> "Where there is division and wrath, God does not dwell."

- **Early Church Texts**: earlychurchtexts.com/main/ignatius/ignatius.shtml
- **CCEL**: ccel.org/ccel/richardson/fathers.vi.ii.html

## Polycarp of Smyrna

### Basic Information

**Author**: Polycarp, Bishop of Smyrna  
**Date**: Letter ~110-140 AD; Martyrdom 155 or 167 AD  
**Genre**: Pastoral letter; martyrdom account  
**Connection**: Disciple of John the Apostle; teacher of Irenaeus  
**Significance**: Bridge between Apostolic and later patristic era

### The Letter to the Philippians

**Occasion**: Responding to the Philippians' request after hosting Ignatius  
**Themes**: Righteousness, faith, endurance, church order

| Theme | Chapters | Key Points |
|-------|----------|------------|
| Faith, Hope, Love | 1-3 | Endurance in righteousness |
| Widows, Deacons, Presbyters | 4-6 | Church order instructions |
| Against Docetism | 7 | "Whoever does not confess Christ come in the flesh is antichrist" |
| Endurance | 8-9 | Examples of Paul, Ignatius |
| Valens's Fall | 11 | Warning against avarice |
| Prayer for All | 12 | Even for enemies |

**Key Passage (7.1)**:
> "For everyone who does not confess that Jesus Christ has come in the flesh is an antichrist; and whoever does not confess the testimony of the Cross is of the devil."

### Martyrdom of Polycarp

**Date**: 155 or 167 AD  
**Genre**: Earliest Christian martyrdom account  
**Significance**: Model for later martyrologies

| Episode | Chapter | Description |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Arrest | 5-7 | Polycarp refuses to flee; feeds captors |
| At Stadium | 9-11 | "86 years have I served him" |
| Prayer | 14 | Beautiful Eucharistic-style prayer |
| Death | 15-16 | Fire forms vault; stabbed; blood extinguishes fire |
| Body Gathered | 17-18 | Bones more precious than gold |

**Famous Exchange (9.3)**:
> Proconsul: "Swear by the fortune of Caesar... say 'Away with the atheists.'"  
> Polycarp (gesturing to the crowd): "Away with the atheists!"  
> Proconsul: "Revile Christ."  
> Polycarp: "Eighty-six years have I served him, and he has done me no wrong. How can I blaspheme my King and my Savior?"

- **Early Church Texts**: earlychurchtexts.com/main/polycarp/polycarp.shtml

## The Didache (Teaching of the Twelve Apostles)

### Basic Information

**Date**: ~50-120 AD (likely late 1st century)  
**Genre**: Church manual / catechetical handbook  
**Provenance**: Syria or Egypt  
**Discovery**: Found 1873 by Philotheos Bryennios in Constantinople  
**Significance**: Window into earliest church practice

### Structure & Content

| Section | Chapters | Content |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **Two Ways** | 1-6 | Moral instruction: Way of Life, Way of Death |
| **Liturgical** | 7-10 | Baptism, fasting, Eucharist |
| **Church Order** | 11-15 | Prophets, apostles, bishops, deacons |
| **Eschatological** | 16 | Watch; signs before the End |

### Major Themes & Key Passages

#### The Two Ways (Moral Catechesis)

**1.1-2**:
> "There are two ways, one of life and one of death, and there is a great difference between the two ways. Now this is the way of life: first, you shall love God who made you; second, your neighbor as yourself."

**2-4**: Detailed moral instruction (reflects Decalogue)

**5**: The Way of Death (list of sins)

#### Baptism (Chapter 7)

> "Baptize in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, in living [running] water. But if you have no living water, baptize in other water... If you have neither, pour water on the head three times..."

**Fasting before baptism**: Both baptizer and baptized should fast.

#### Fasting & Prayer (Chapter 8)

> "Do not let your fasts be with the hypocrites, for they fast on Monday and Thursday; but you shall fast on Wednesday and Friday."

**Lord's Prayer**: Prayed three times daily. Full text given.

#### Eucharist (Chapters 9-10)

**9.1-5** (Before meal):
> "Concerning the Eucharist, give thanks thus. First for the cup: 'We thank you, our Father, for the holy vine of David your servant, which you made known to us through Jesus your servant...'"

> "And concerning the broken bread: 'We thank you, our Father, for the life and knowledge which you made known to us through Jesus your servant...'"

**9.5** (Restriction):
> "Let no one eat or drink of your Eucharist except those baptized in the Lord's name."

**10** (After meal): Thanksgiving prayer with Maranatha ("Our Lord, come!")

#### Church Order (Chapters 11-15)

**11-13**: Testing prophets and apostles (traveling ministers)

**14.1** (Sunday Eucharist):
> "On the Lord's Day of the Lord come together, break bread, and give thanks, having first confessed your sins, that your sacrifice may be pure."

**15.1** (Bishops and Deacons):
> "Appoint for yourselves bishops and deacons worthy of the Lord... for they also minister to you the ministry of the prophets and teachers."

#### Eschatology (Chapter 16)

Watch and be ready; false prophets will come; Antichrist; resurrection of the holy ones.

### Where to Find Full Text

- **New Advent**: newadvent.org/fathers/0714.htm
- **Early Church Texts**: earlychurchtexts.com/main/didache/didache.shtml
- **CCEL**: ccel.org/ccel/richardson/fathers.viii.html

## Epistle of Barnabas

### Basic Information

**Author**: Unknown (not the biblical Barnabas)  
**Date**: ~70-132 AD (likely ~96-100 AD)  
**Genre**: Theological treatise/letter  
**Provenance**: Alexandria (Egypt) likely  
**Significance**: Extensive OT typology; Two Ways section

### Structure & Content

| Section | Chapters | Content |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **Faith & Covenant** | 1-5 | Understanding God's covenant |
| **OT Typology** | 6-12 | Sacrifices, scapegoat, red heifer, circumcision, Sabbath |
| **Jesus in OT** | 12-14 | Cross prefigured; Jesus as Lord of Sabbath |
| **Temple** | 16 | Spiritual temple in us |
| **Two Ways** | 18-20 | Light vs. Darkness (similar to Didache) |

### Key Themes

#### Typological Interpretation

Barnabas sees Christ everywhere in the OT:

- **Red heifer** (ch. 8): Type of Christ's sacrifice
- **Scapegoat** (ch. 7): Bearing our sins
- **Circumcision** (ch. 9): Spiritual meaning, not literal
- **318 servants of Abraham** (ch. 9): T (300) = Cross; IH (18) = Jesus

#### Against Literal Judaism

Barnabas argues the Jews misunderstood their own Scriptures, taking literally what was meant spiritually. (This supersessionist reading is historically important but pastorally problematic.)

#### The True Temple (Chapter 16)

> "Before we believed in God, the dwelling place of our heart was corrupt and weak... but when we received remission of sins... the Lord himself dwells in us."

## Shepherd of Hermas

### Basic Information

**Author**: Hermas (Roman Christian)  
**Date**: ~100-160 AD (likely mid-2nd century)  
**Genre**: Apocalyptic visions with moral instruction  
**Setting**: Rome  
**Significance**: One of most popular early Christian writings; nearly canonical

### Structure

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Visions** (1-4) | Lady (Church) appears; tower being built |
| **Vision 5 / Mandate intro** | Shepherd (Angel of Repentance) appears |
| **Mandates** (1-12) | Twelve moral commandments |
| **Similitudes** (1-10) | Parables and allegories |

### Key Themes

#### The Church as Tower (Vision 3)

Church is a tower being built with stones (believers). Some stones fit perfectly; others must be trimmed; some are rejected.

#### Post-Baptismal Repentance

**Mandate 4.3** (Crucial for penitential theology):
> "I have heard from certain teachers that there is no second repentance beyond that one when we went down into the water... He said to me, 'You have heard correctly... But for those who have been already called before these days, the Lord has appointed repentance.'"

**One more chance**: Hermas teaches there is one (and only one) additional repentance possible after baptism—important for later penitential debates.

#### Twelve Mandates

1. Faith in one God
2. Simplicity, innocence
3. Truth
4. Chastity
5. Patience, long-suffering
6. Two spirits (good angel, evil angel)
7. Fear the Lord, not the devil
8. Self-control
9. Do not doubt
10. Put away grief
11. False vs. true prophets
12. Evil desire vs. good desire

#### The Willow Tree (Similitude 8)

Various branches: some dry (apostates), some half-dry (double-minded), some green (faithful)—degrees of faithfulness.

### Where to Find Full Text

- **New Advent**: newadvent.org/fathers/0201.htm (Visions), 0202.htm (Commands), 0203.htm (Similitudes)
- **Early Church Texts**: earlychurchtexts.com/main/hermas/hermas.shtml
- **CCEL**: ccel.org/ccel/richardson/fathers.x.html

## 2 Clement

### Basic Information

**Author**: Unknown (not Clement of Rome)  
**Date**: ~140-160 AD  
**Genre**: Homily (earliest surviving Christian sermon)  
**Provenance**: Corinth or Rome  
**Significance**: Shows early preaching style; high Christology

### Key Themes

#### Christ's Divinity

**2 Clement 1.1**:
> "Brothers, we ought to think of Jesus Christ as we think of God, as the judge of the living and the dead."

#### The Pre-existent Church

**2 Clement 14** (Remarkable passage):
> "The Church... existed before the sun and moon... she was created first of all things... The living Church is the body of Christ."

#### Call to Repentance

Repeated exhortation to repent while there is time.

## Epistle to Diognetus

### Basic Information

**Author**: Unknown  
**Date**: ~150-200 AD  
**Genre**: Apologetic letter  
**Audience**: Diognetus (possibly an inquirer or official)  
**Significance**: Beautiful description of Christian distinctiveness

### Structure

| Section | Chapters | Content |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Pagan critique | 1-2 | Idolatry's absurdity |
| Jewish critique | 3-4 | Ritual inadequacy |
| Christian distinctiveness | 5-6 | Citizens of heaven |
| Mystery of salvation | 7-9 | God's plan in Christ |
| Exhortation | 10-12 | Become imitator of God |

### Key Passage (Chapters 5-6)

The most beautiful patristic description of Christian life:

> "Christians are not distinguished from the rest of mankind either in locality or in speech or in customs... They dwell in their own countries, but only as sojourners; they bear their share in all things as citizens, and they endure all hardships as strangers. Every foreign country is a fatherland to them, and every fatherland is foreign."

> "They are in the flesh, but they do not live after the flesh. They live on earth, but their citizenship is in heaven... They love all men, and they are persecuted by all."

> "What the soul is in the body, that the Christians are in the world."

## Papias of Hierapolis

### Basic Information

**Author**: Papias, Bishop of Hierapolis  
**Date**: fl. ~110-130 AD  
**Work**: *Expositions of the Sayings of the Lord* (5 books, lost)  
**Significance**: Reports about Gospel origins; knew Polycarp; heard "John the Elder"

### Surviving Fragments (via Eusebius, Irenaeus)

#### On Gospel Origins

**On Mark** (Eusebius, *Hist. Eccl.* III.39.15):
> "Mark, having become the interpreter of Peter, wrote down accurately, though not in order, whatsoever he remembered of the things said or done by Christ."

**On Matthew** (III.39.16):
> "Matthew composed the sayings (logia) in the Hebrew language, and everyone interpreted them as he was able."

#### On John the Elder

Papias distinguishes between John the Apostle and "John the Elder"—important for authorship debates.

#### Millennial Views

Papias held chiliastic (millennial) views about Christ's earthly kingdom—Eusebius criticizes him for this.

### Where to Find Fragments

- **New Advent**: newadvent.org/fathers/0125.htm
- **Eusebius, Church History**: Book III, chapters 39-40
